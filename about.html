How Nuclear Power Generation Works

Understanding the Science Behind South Carolina’s Nuclear Energy

Nuclear energy provides more than half of South Carolina’s electricity. But how does it actually work? Nuclear power plants generate electricity using a process called nuclear fission, which releases energy from atoms.

Nuclear Fission:
Splitting the Atom

At the core of nuclear power generation is uranium, a naturally occurring element.

  • Nuclear power plants use uranium fuel, typically uranium-235.
  • When a uranium atom is struck by a neutron, it splits.
  • This splitting process is called fission.
  • Fission releases:
    • A large amount of heat energy
    • More neutrons
    • Smaller atoms (called fission products)

The released neutrons strike other uranium atoms, creating a controlled chain reaction inside the reactor.

The Nuclear Reactor

The reactor is the heart of a nuclear power plant. Inside the reactor:

  • Fuel rods contain small uranium pellets.
  • Control rods absorb neutrons to regulate the reaction.
  • The chain reaction produces heat.
  • Water circulates through the reactor to absorb this heat.

Control rods can be raised or lowered to control how fast the reaction happens. This ensures the process remains safe and stable.

Turning Heat Into Steam

The heat generated in the reactor is used to boil water.

  • Heated water turns into high-pressure steam.
  • The steam moves through pipes toward a turbine.
  • In most South Carolina plants, the system is designed so that radioactive materials remain contained within secure systems.

This step is similar to how many other power plants (coal or natural gas) generate electricity — the difference is the heat source.

Spinning the Turbine and Generator

The high-pressure steam spins a large turbine.

  • The turbine is connected to a generator.
  • As the turbine spins, the generator produces electricity.
  • Electricity is then sent through power lines to homes and businesses across South Carolina.

After passing through the turbine, the steam is cooled, condensed back into water, and reused in the system.

Cooling Systems

You may recognize nuclear plants by their large cooling towers.

  • Cooling towers release water vapor, not smoke.
  • The vapor you see is similar to steam from a cloud.
  • Cooling systems remove excess heat to keep the plant operating efficiently.

South Carolina’s plants are often located near lakes or rivers to support cooling needs.
